Market Updates and Growth Trends

Used Serviceable Material (USM) Market Faces Intensifying Competition, Prompting New Investment Strategies

  • The aviation aftermarket's USM (Used Serviceable Material) segment is experiencing heightened competitive dynamics as more companies enter the market for purchasing aircraft and engines for teardown (Aviation Week, April 2)
  • Younger platforms being dismantled: Airbus A320neo-family aircraft are increasingly being acquired for teardown at younger ages than historically seen, signalling strong demand for USM parts even from next-generation platforms
  • Extended fleet life paradox: Ongoing new-aircraft production delays and supply chain constraints are pushing airlines to extend older platform service lives, simultaneously increasing demand for USM from legacy fleets (e.g., classic CFM56, older A320 family) while younger aircraft are also entering the teardown pipeline
  • Investment implications: Market participants are adjusting investment strategies to compete effectively — including sourcing agreements, inventory positioning, and MRO partnership models
  • Why it matters: USM is a critical supply valve for the MRO industry, especially during periods of new-build scarcity. The dual pressure of younger teardowns and extended legacy fleet operations is creating a complex market dynamic that will shape parts availability and pricing through 2026–2027

Engine MRO Market to Reach $50.67B in 2026, Growing at 4% CAGR

  • The global Aircraft Engine MRO Market is valued at USD 50.67 billion in 2026, projected to reach USD 61.66 billion by 2031 (Mordor Intelligence, February 2026)
  • General Electric remains the largest engine MRO player, followed by Rolls-Royce, CFM International, and Pratt & Whitney
  • Key growth drivers: rising aircraft utilisation rates post-pandemic, aging widebody fleets entering heavy maintenance windows, and LEAP-1A/1B fleet maturity
  • Why it matters: The 4% CAGR represents a significant expansion of total addressable MRO spend, but the market is constrained by capacity — meaning pricing power is shifting toward MRO providers who can actually deliver

Regional Expansion Activities

Ontic Invests $30M in Global MRO Infrastructure — Miramar Operational, Tewksbury Coming

  • Ontic (45+ years in the OEM aftermarket) announced a $30 million global MRO infrastructure investment ahead of MRO Americas 2026, funding two purpose-built facilities (Aviation Talk / LinkedIn, April 4)
  • Miramar, Florida (USA): Fully operational — serving as Ontic's primary US MRO hub, consolidating teams, equipment, and processes under one roof
  • Tewksbury, United Kingdom: Phased introduction underway; full operational capacity expected September 2026 — expanding Ontic's European footprint
  • Investment objectives: Shorter turnaround times, greater transparency for customers, and OEM-certified repairs with extended component life
  • Strategic focus: Ontic is positioning itself as the solution provider for operators facing obsolescence management, legacy fleet support, and supply chain risk mitigation — the very challenges intensifying as fleet ages diversify
  • MRO Americas 2026 preview: Ontic will exhibit at Stand 2903, N-S Hall, Orlando April 21–23, with new partnership announcements expected

Hanwha Aviation Scales Up After Eko Green MRO Acquisition

  • Hanwha Aviation CEO Jeff Lewis outlined the company's evolving MRO strategy in an Aviation Week interview (April 2, Lee Ann Shay / James Pozzi)
  • Hanwha Aviation launched as an engine leasing platform in 2024, expanded in 2025 by acquiring Eko Green (an MRO company), and is now actively building out its aftermarket capabilities
  • The focus is on post-merger integration — aligning Eko Green's operations with Hanwha's broader engine leasing and lifecycle management platform
  • Why it matters: Hanwha represents a new category of entrant in the MRO/aftermarket space — backed by a major industrial conglomerate and leveraging the engine leasing model as a customer acquisition vehicle for MRO services

Florida's MRO Ecosystem Gathers Momentum Ahead of MRO Americas

  • New investments are accelerating Florida's emergence as a major US MRO hub, building toward MRO Americas 2026 in Orlando (Aviation Week, April 1)
  • Florida benefits from a cluster of MRO providers, aerospace defence contractors, training institutions, and a favourable tax and regulatory environment — all converging as the April 21–23 event draws near
  • Why it matters: With MRO Americas anchoring the industry calendar in Orlando, Florida's ecosystem is becoming a year-round MRO destination, attracting investment and talent from across the Americas

Asia-Pacific Engine MRO: Scarcity Meets Supply Chain Strain

  • Aviation Week's April 2 deep-dive confirmed the Asia-Pacific region continues to face a dual bottleneck of capacity scarcity and supply chain disruption in engine MRO (Aviation Week, April 2)
  • Asia-Pacific accounts for a significant and growing share of global engine MRO spend, driven by rapid fleet expansion across China, India, Southeast Asia, and Australia
  • Engine shop visit wait times in the region mirror global trends: 6–18 months for major engine types, with GTF, LEAP, and Trent family backlogs all contributing
  • Why it matters: Asia-Pacific's capacity gap is prompting airlines to route engines internationally, increasing logistics costs and cycle times — creating both a challenge and an opportunity for global MRO providers

Supply Chain Developments

GE Aerospace Reorganization Unites Engines + Services Under One Roof GE Aerospace

  • GE Aerospace has reorganized its technology and operations divisions into a single, expanded Commercial Engines & Services organization under the leadership of President & CEO Mohamed Ali (Aviation Week, April 3, Guy Norris)
  • The reorganization — executed less than two years after GE Aerospace became an independent entity — is designed to accelerate aftermarket expansion by integrating engine programme knowledge with MRO delivery
  • $1B MRO capacity investment confirmed: GE Aerospace has committed $1 billion to expanding MRO facilities and capabilities globally; approximately half allocated specifically to the LEAP-1 engine aftermarket within its service network
  • The LEAP engine (produced by CFM International, a GE-Safran 50/50 JV) is now entering its first major heavy-maintenance wave — making the timing of GE's capacity investment critical
  • Why it matters: GE's reorganization signals a more unified commercial posture in the MRO aftermarket — the combined engines + services structure gives GE deeper control over the full engine lifecycle, from OEM support to shop visit execution

CFM Buttresses LEAP and CFM56 Support as Dual-Demand Surge Hits

  • CFM International (GE Aerospace + Safran) is simultaneously ramping LEAP production for new Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX deliveries while heavily investing in aftermarket support for both LEAP and the legacy CFM56 family (Aviation Week, April 3, Sean Broderick / Thierry Dubois)
  • The narrative that LEAP ramp-up would detract from CFM56 aftermarket attention has proven false — CFM is "pouring resources" into its MRO ecosystem for both engine families simultaneously
  • Ryanair case study: CFM is working directly with Ryanair to support two dedicated engine overhaul shops operated by the airline, enabling rapid in-house turnaround at scale
  • CFM56 remains one of the most widely deployed engine families globally (~15,000+ delivered); the dual-support commitment is critical for operators of mixed fleets
  • Why it matters: The CFM model — maintaining legacy support while scaling new engine capacity — is the template other engine OEMs are watching closely as LEAP, GTF, and Trent 7000 fleets mature in parallel

AI Power Demand Creates Unexpected Engine MRO Dynamic

  • A striking structural shift is emerging: industrial gas turbines derived from aviation engines — specifically the GE LM2500 (derived from the CF6) — are being deployed to power AI data centres amid the explosive growth of generative AI infrastructure (Aviation Week, April 3, Sean Broderick)
  • This "AI power grab" for turbine engines could theoretically divert capacity from aviation MRO — but the article identifies a dual positive for engine suppliers:

1. Expanded market for industrial engine variants increases production scale, potentially reducing unit costs

2. Shared component sourcing and technology development across aviation and industrial applications benefits both

  • Why it matters: The intersection of AI infrastructure buildout and aerospace supply chains is a new structural variable that MRO providers and engine OEMs must factor into capacity planning

Component OEMs Are Pursuing Direct MRO Growth Strategies

  • The trend of component OEM manufacturers expanding into MRO services continued to accelerate (Aviation Week, April 2)
  • Rather than relying solely on independent MRO networks, component OEMs (e.g., landing gear, avionics, pneumatics) are building or acquiring MRO capabilities to capture aftermarket margin and ensure quality control
  • Why it matters: This vertical integration trend could reshape independent MRO competitive dynamics — operators may face more sole-source component MRO situations, increasing pricing and availability risk

Technology and Investment News

SAS Achieves ~1,000 Predictive Maintenance Work Orders Post-Restructuring

  • SAS (Scandinavian Airlines), navigating a post-restructuring environment, has quietly built a significant predictive maintenance capability centred on Airbus RMAX technology (Aviation Week, March 31, Lindsay Bjerregaard)
  • Deployment: SAS retrofitted its older fleet — including A330 widebody aircraft — with Airbus RMAX sensor and analytics systems to capture real-time engine and systems health data
  • Results: Since going live, SAS has generated approximately 1,000 predictive maintenance work orders, enabling proactive component replacement and reducing unscheduled maintenance events
  • The SAS example demonstrates that predictive maintenance ROI is achievable even on mixed, older-generation fleets — not only new-build aircraft with embedded digital capabilities
  • Why it matters: Predictive maintenance is no longer a bleeding-edge experiment — it is becoming operational necessity for airlines seeking to reduce AOG exposure and optimise parts inventory in a constrained supply chain environment

Intensified Labor Competition Driving New MRO Workforce Strategies

  • MRO providers are ramping up capacity to meet surging demand, but labor availability has become the binding constraint — prompting a wave of new workforce strategies (Aviation Week, April 3, Lindsay Bjerregaard)

  • AAR Corp. example: AAR plans to add 200 new employees — illustrative of aggressive headcount scaling underway across the sector
  • Why it matters: The technician shortage is structural, not cyclical — driven by years of underinvestment in aviation career pathways. Providers who win the workforce race will win the capacity race

Major Challenges and Solutions

Fleet Extension vs. USM Supply: The Paradox Reshaping MRO Planning

  • The simultaneous pressure of new aircraft production delays (forcing operators to keep older aircraft flying longer) and younger aircraft entering the teardown market creates a counterintuitive planning challenge for MRO providers and parts buyers:
  • Extended legacy fleets require more USM from classic platforms (A320ceo, 737Classic/NG, older regional jets)
  • Younger aircraft teardowns (A320neo, newer types) are pulling inventory that historically would have been reserved for longer service
  • Solution: Investment in engine trend monitoring and predictive life management to maximise residual life of components, reducing dependence on teardown supply

Engine Capacity Shortage Remains the Industry's Defining Constraint

  • Bain & Company's 2024 research — now confirmed in 2026 market data — established that 2026 is the peak year for commercial engine MRO demand, with capacity severely constrained across all major engine types:
  • GTF (Pratt & Whitney): Groundings and recall programmes continue to affect fleet availability; MRO capacity for GTF repairs still scaling
  • LEAP-1A/1B: First major heavy-check wave beginning; shop visit queues extending to 6–12 months
  • Trent 700/7000: Widebody engine backlogs extending 12–18 months for some operators
  • CFM56-7B: Mature fleet, but significant shop visit demand from Southwest, Ryanair, and other high-cycle operators
  • Solution pathway: Engine OEMs (CFM, GE, RTX) and independent MROs are all investing simultaneously — GE's $1B commitment being the largest single signal. But capacity takes 2–4 years to come online

Workforce Shortage: Structural Risk to MRO Capacity Expansion

  • The investment in new MRO facilities (Florida, Singapore, Europe, Asia-Pacific) is running ahead of the workforce to staff them
  • DEI policy shifts and competition from non-aerospace industries (tech, defence, automotive) are complicating recruiting
  • Gig-style technician labour models are emerging as a partial solution — but raise questions about quality consistency and regulatory compliance
  • Solutions in play: International recruiting, school-to-MRO pipelines, competitive compensation, and automation/AI augmentation to increase technician productivity
