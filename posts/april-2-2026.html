MRO Industry Q2 2026 Outlook: Record Demand Meets Structural Constraint

  • Global commercial MRO spend tracking at record levels heading into Q2 2026, with demand driven by:
  • Aging in-service fleet extending beyond planned retirement ages due to OEM delivery shortfalls (17,000+ aircraft backlog; 5,300+ cumulative delivery shortfall through 2025–26)
  • Post-COVID passenger traffic running at and above 2019 levels in most regions
  • Engine MRO remaining the most acute bottleneck, with GTF average shop visit times still at ~300 days versus ~60 days pre-pandemic
  • MRO Global Market Forecast 2026–2034: Industry analysts project the global aviation MRO market will reach $61.5 billion by 2033, growing at ~6.5% CAGR from approximately $36 billion in 2026
  • Key structural constraint: Demand is not the problem; the binding constraint is the industry's collective ability to deliver maintenance on time and at scale, limited by certified technician shortages, parts lead times, and shop-visit capacity ceilings

Regional Expansion Activities

easyJet CEO Sets 50% In-House Heavy Maintenance Target — Plans Acquisition of Adria Tehnika Facility in Slovenia

  • easyJet CEO Kenton Jarvis confirmed on March 31, 2026 (Brussels) that the airline is actively targeting a 50% in-house heavy maintenance ratio — up from the current 25% — as a strategic lever to:
  • Reduce dependency on third-party MRO providers during periods of capacity shortage
  • Create internal cost benchmarks to pressure external suppliers on pricing
  • Integrate predictive maintenance capabilities more tightly with scheduling
  • Adria Tehnika acquisition: easyJet is finalising the purchase of the Adria Tehnika heavy maintenance facility at Ljubljana Brnik Airport, Slovenia — a five-bay heavy check hangar that has supported easyJet's Airbus fleet for years; the deal (announced December 2025) is expected to close early Q2 2026, subject to regulatory approval
  • easyJet's existing in-house MRO footprint:
  • Malta facility (acquired from SR Technics, completed May 2024): accommodates heavy maintenance for the airline's 340+ aircraft fleet with a 400+ workforce
  • With Adria Tehnika added, easyJet will control two dedicated heavy maintenance hangars, supporting the push toward 50% insourcing
  • Engine overhaul stance: Despite the insourcing trend, easyJet has no immediate plans to perform its own engine overhaul work — CEO Jarvis acknowledged the logic of rival Ryanair's engine self-service strategy but described it as "not our priority" for now
  • Industry significance: easyJet's insourcing push is part of a wider trend among LCCs reassessing their make-vs-buy calculus as third-party MRO capacity remains tight and costs elevated

Source: Aviation Week Network (Victoria Moores), March 31–April 1, 2026; CAPA Centre for Aviation, April 1, 2026

Supply Chain Developments

Airbus Launches Damages Claim Against Pratt & Whitney Over GTF Engine Shortages — "Glider" Backlog Exceeds Industry Tolerance Aviation Week

  • Airbus SE formally launched a damages claim against Pratt & Whitney (RTX) in March 2026 — the most significant OEM-supplier legal confrontation in commercial aviation since the pandemic
  • Core dispute: The PW1000G Geared Turbofan (GTF) engine's prolonged supply crisis, rooted in a powder metal contamination defect disclosed mid-2023 requiring ~1,200 engine recalls, with repair timelines far exceeding initial commitments
  • Airbus's documented impact:
  • Hundreds of "glider" aircraft — completed A320neo and A220 fuselages awaiting engine installation — unable to be delivered
  • Airbus Q1 2026 deliveries: 54 aircraft vs. 65 aircraft in Q1 2025 — a ~20% year-over-year decline
  • Airbus's target of 75 aircraft/month for the A320neo family pushed back to late 2027
  • Airbus stock down ~20% in 2026 to date
  • Airbus has paid "hundreds of millions of dollars" in delay penalties to airline customers
  • Pratt & Whitney's position:
  • RTX paid approximately $1 billion in GTF-related compensation charges in 2025
  • P&W maintains it has been prioritising MRO throughput on grounded fleet before new engine deliveries — which Airbus disputes
  • P&W's GTF Enhancement Programme targets 90–95% improvement in engine durability via 35 component upgrades, with completion of in-service fleet upgrades expected through 2026
  • Affected aircraft: The A220 series is entirely dependent on P&W PW1500G engines; ~40% of A320neo aircraft use GTF (vs. 60% CFM LEAP-1A)
  • Industry-wide MRO implication: The legal dispute highlights the systemic risk when a single OEM supplier becomes a structural bottleneck for both new-aircraft delivery AND in-service MRO support simultaneously

Source: AeronauticsMagazine.com (Marius Bujor), March 31, 2026; RTX investor relations

Boeing Global Services Invests in AI, USM, and Inventory to Counter Persistent Supply Chain Gaps

  • Boeing Global Services (BGS) told Aviation Week (March 31, 2026) that while the supply chain is "starting to stabilise," a full recovery to pre-pandemic benchmarks is unlikely before 2030
  • Key supply chain pressures Boeing identified:
  • Supplier capacity limitations constraining demand fulfilment
  • Unexpected shortages from obsolescence, non-conforming parts, and sub-component gaps within OEM supply chains
  • BGS's three-pronged response:

1. AI and Data Analytics:

  • Parts Planning Hub: data-driven collaborative tool improving demand forecasting accuracy for spare parts
  • Landing gear analytics tool: uses aircraft health monitoring data to predict component replacement needs ahead of shop visits

2. Inventory and Logistics Expansion:

  • Increased investment in transactional spares, component repair, and exchange programmes
  • Expanding global warehouse network — moving stock closer to airline and MRO customers

3. Used Serviceable Material (USM) Market:

  • BGS projects the USM market will grow at ~10% per year
  • USM enables operators to optimise maintenance strategies, reduce costs, and sustain airworthiness without sacrificing quality standards
  • Particularly critical for airlines managing extended fleet life beyond original retirement plans
  • Upcoming coverage: Full BGS and major component OEM aftermarket strategy analysis to be published in the next edition of Inside MRO (Aviation Week)

Source: Aviation Week Network (Alex Derber), March 31, 2026

Southwest Airlines Faces CFM56 and LEAP-1B Spare Engine Crunch — H2 2026 HPT Wave Looming

  • Southwest Airlines outlined its near-term engine MRO challenge in an April 1, 2026 Aviation Week interview with VP of Maintenance Ken Barone
  • Southwest's engine portfolio: ~800 engines across CFM56-7B (737-700/800) and LEAP-1B (737 MAX)
  • Two convergent MRO waves in H2 2026:
  • CFM56 P04 HPT blade inspection programme — a wave of high-pressure turbine blade replacements creating simultaneous shop visit demand across multiple fleets
  • LEAP-1B Stage 1 HPT disc life limit (≤10,000 cycles) — disc replacement requirements triggering new engine inductions across the growing MAX fleet
  • Southwest's spare engine concern: "Spare engine availability is one of our most significant worries heading into the second half of the year" — availability of on-wing spares is critical to maintaining aircraft readiness
  • MRO partner network: Southwest relies on StandardAero, SR Technics, Lufthansa Technik, MTU Maintenance for engine overhaul alongside CFM's and GE Aerospace's own PBH (power-by-the-hour) arrangements
  • MAX transition update: Boeing 737 MAX 7 certification expected in 2026, with Southwest planning entry into service in 2027
  • Financial strength as buffer: Southwest owns 88% of its airframes and engines outright — providing strategic flexibility in fleet and engine management unavailable to highly-leased carriers

Source: Aviation Week Network (Lee Ann Shay, James Pozzi), April 1, 2026

FAA Launches Rulemaking to Recognise Foreign Maintenance Certificates — Bilateral Agreement Framework to Cut Duplicate Audits

  • The FAA has formally initiated a rulemaking process (published March 31, 2026) to enable recognition of non-U.S. maintenance organisation certificates through bilateral aviation safety agreements
  • Core change: Under the proposed framework, the FAA could directly accept EASA-certificated, Transport Canada-certificated, and other bilaterally agreed MRO organisations' approvals — eliminating the need for a full separate FAA approval process
  • Industry benefit: Significantly reduces duplicative regulatory audit burden and cost for non-U.S. MRO providers seeking access to the US market — a long-sought reform by ARSA and international MRO associations
  • Current status: Rule in early-to-mid rulemaking stage; exact timeline for implementation not yet disclosed
  • Workforce context: The FAA simultaneously projects demand for 190,000 new certified aviation mechanics by 2037 — with training pipeline currently insufficient to close the gap; the foreign certificate recognition rule is seen as one tool for addressing skilled-labour scarcity by expanding the accessible workforce pool

Source: Aviation Week Network (Sean Broderick), March 31, 2026; Aviation Technician Shortage analysis, OxMaint, March 2026

Major Challenges and Solutions

Challenge: MRO Workforce Shortage — FAA Projects 190,000 New Mechanics Needed by 2037

  • The aviation MRO industry faces a structural workforce crisis that hiring budgets alone cannot resolve:
  • Demand side: FAA projects 190,000 new certified A&P mechanics needed by 2037; global estimates put the shortfall at 300,000+ technicians across commercial, business, and military aviation
  • Supply side: Aviation mechanic school enrollments recovering post-COVID but still ~30% below 2019 levels; training-to-certification cycle takes 18–24 months minimum
  • Retention challenge: Experienced technicians leaving MRO for manufacturing roles offering comparable pay without the shift/rotational schedule burden
  • MRO sector responses in Q1–Q2 2026:
  • India: Aviation Maintenance Engineering (AME) institute launches apprentice AME programme with IndiGo — first major carrier-sponsored AME training pipeline in India's domestic MRO sector
  • Scandinavia: Revitalised SAS (post-restructuring) reports early success with predictive maintenance programmes that reduce unscheduled maintenance events — partially offsetting technician shortage by reducing emergency work volume
  • MRO Americas 2026: Dedicated sessions on mentorship, high-school-to-MRO pipeline, and "soft skills gap" among new-hire technicians — reflecting industry recognition that workforce development is an existential issue, not just an HR challenge
  • High school MRO competition finding (Aviation Week, April 2026): Aviation Week coverage highlighted that high-school students in MRO skills competitions are performing at parity with or exceeding professionals in certain standardised tasks — suggesting that the next generation of technicians is emerging from non-traditional pathways

Source: Aviation Week Network; OxMaint Aviation Technician Shortage 2026 analysis; MRO Business Today, April 1, 2026

Challenge: USM Market Expansion vs. OEM Spare Parts Strategy

  • The Used Serviceable Material (USM) market is accelerating as airlines and MROs seek cost-effective alternatives to new OEM spares, which face long lead times and elevated prices:
  • Boeing Global Services projecting USM market growing at ~10% annually; expanding its own USM consignment and resale capabilities
  • AerSale (a leading independent USM trader) has flagged rising asset acquisition costs as competition for used aircraft and engine teardowns intensifies — reflecting the market's maturation
  • Setna iO acquires J&C Aero — expanding cabin interior component services capabilities, reflecting the growing importance of cabin MRO as airlines refresh interiors without awaiting new aircraft delivery
  • OEM vs. PMA/USM tension: Southwest Airlines confirmed it has no plans to use PMA (Parts Manufacturer Approval) parts — maintaining an OEM-only strategy; but the broader industry trend is toward mixed strategies as lead times for OEM parts extend beyond operator tolerances

Source: Aviation Week MRO Inside Briefs March–April 2026; Boeing Global Services, March 31, 2026; ePlane AI analysis

Industry Outlook

Q2 2026 Opens with Legal Escalation and Structural Pressure — But Also Acceleration:

April 2, 2026 finds the MRO industry entering Q2 with a backdrop of escalating legal tension (Airbus vs. Pratt & Whitney), persistent supply chain constraints, and a workforce shortfall that will take years to close. Yet simultaneously, the pace of investment, capacity expansion, and technology deployment in 2026 is unprecedented.

Three themes dominate the next 19 days before MRO Americas opens:

1. Legal and commercial pressure from Airbus–P&W dispute will force RTX to accelerate its delivery prioritisation commitments and GTF MRO throughput — positive for the overall GTF in-service fleet but creating ongoing delivery drama for new A220 and A320neo operators.

2. In-sourcing vs. outsourcing realignment — easyJet's 50% in-house target joins Ryanair as evidence that LCCs with scale are increasingly moving to vertical integration of maintenance, while smaller regional operators rely more on OEM-supported programmes (Embraer's E-Jet support model).

3. Technology investment as the long-term solution — GE Aerospace's AI-driven 21-day cycle target in Singapore, P&W's robotics deployment at Eagle Services Asia, and Boeing's AI-powered Parts Planning Hub collectively signal that MRO's structural constraints will ultimately be solved by automation and predictive intelligence — but the timeline stretches to 2027–2028, not 2026.

MRO Americas (April 21–23) is now 19 days away. All eyes will be on what GE Aerospace, CFM/Safran, P&W/RTX, and the major MRO integrators announce in the pre-show window.
