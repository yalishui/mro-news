Market Updates and Growth Trends

HEICO / Sherwood Avionics Acquisition Closes — Defense MRO Expansion Takes Shape

  • Details emerge following the April 7 HEICO announcement on Sherwood Avionics: the acquisition reinforces HEICO's Flight Support Group (FSG) strategy of acquiring niche, high-expertise Part 145 operators at scale (MRO Business Today, April 8; AviTrader, April 8)
  • Sherwood's ~150-person workforce and established OEM relationships across C-130, F-15, F-16, CH-47, and UH-60 platforms give HEICO direct access to US DoD and allied nation defense MRO contracts
  • Market context: Defense MRO globally is entering an expansion phase — NATO allies increasing defence spending to 2–3% of GDP targets, with aircraft fleet readiness a priority across multiple air forces
  • Why it matters: HEICO's move into defense APU and component overhaul (Sherwood's core capabilities) complements its commercial PMA parts dominance — the company is methodically building a dual-track portfolio that spans both civil and military aftermarket

Setna iO Credit Expansion Amplifies in Context — Aftermarket Capital Cycle Heating Up

  • The $550 million Setna iO facility (announced April 7) is generating market discussion about the leverage dynamics of parts trading platforms (AviTrader, April 8)
  • Key insight: The combination of Setna's Zulu Global South Florida expansion, LGT capability growth, and J&C Aero integration creates a multi-platform MRO + parts ecosystem with interlinked specialisations — engine components (LGT), general trading (PartsLab/Setnix), and aero-structure/USM (Zulu/J&C)
  • Why it matters: Diversified aftermarket platforms are outperforming single-specialisation players in the current market — because no one part of the value chain can fully satisfy the surge demand alone

Regional Expansion Activities

APOC Aviation Acquires A320-200 (MSN 4533) from FTAI for Teardown — USM Supply Reinforced

  • APOC Aviation (Netherlands-based trading and leasing specialist) has acquired Airbus A320-200 MSN 4533 from FTAI Aviation for teardown (APOC press release, April 8; AviTrader, April 9; CAPA, April 8)
  • Aircraft details: 15 years old; most recently operated by Jetstar Pacific Airlines
  • Teardown location: Tarmac Aerosave Toulouse-Francazal facility, France — scheduled for May 2026
  • Purpose: Expand APOC's component supply offering in the V2500 / CFM56 USM market
  • Why APOC: APOC's trading model relies on part-out economics — teardowns generate USM inventory that APOC redistributes to MRO providers and operators. Acquiring a 15-year-old A320 (prime age for teardown) reinforces available stock for the CFM56 and V2500 engine families
  • Market signal: With CFM56 engines in high demand for shop visits across Ryanair, Southwest, easyJet, and other high-cycle operators, teardown of serviceable A320-family aircraft is economically compelling
  • Why it matters: This is the second A320-family teardown announced this week (alongside Executive Jet Support's activities), confirming that the USM supply response to the parts shortage is actively scaling

British School of Aviation Receives EASA Part 147 Approval for A350 / Trent XWB Training

  • British School of Aviation (BSA), operating from London Luton Airport, has received EASA Part 147 approval to deliver training on the Airbus A350 with Rolls-Royce Trent XWB engine (Aviation Week, April 8)
  • Significance: BSA is an approved UK-based maintenance training organisation; the new approval extends its scope to the A350 — a long-haul widebody platform now entering its first major MRO maturity wave
  • Training scope: Category B1 and B2 licensed engineer type training for A350 / Trent XWB, covering both airframe/engine systems integration
  • Why it matters: The A350 fleet is growing rapidly across long-haul operators (Qatar Airways, Cathay Pacific, Lufthansa, Singapore Airlines, Air France, IAG). As these aircraft age toward their first heavy-check cycles, demand for A350-qualified maintenance engineers will accelerate sharply. BSA's certification adds European training capacity ahead of that demand wave

Supply Chain Developments

Widebody Teardown Expansion: Executive Jet Support A340-600 and ERJ-145 Programme

  • Executive Jet Support has acquired multiple aircraft for teardown this week:
  • Two Airbus A340-600s purchased from USC GmbH (Germany); teardown in Poland
  • One Embraer ERJ-145 acquired from Loganair (UK); teardown in Latvia
  • A340-600 context: The quad-engine A340-600 (powered by Rolls-Royce Trent 500s) is increasingly economically unviable to operate — all major operators have retired or are retiring this type. Teardown unlocks high-value Trent 500 components and airframe parts for the remaining A340 fleet
  • ERJ-145 context: Loganair operates a regional UK network; the ERJ-145 retirement feeds the Rolls-Royce AE3007 aftermarket used heavily across regional fleets in North America and Europe
  • Why it matters: Multi-type teardown activity across widebody (A340) and regional jet (ERJ-145) segments in a single week reflects the breadth of USM demand — not just narrowbody CFM56, but also widebody RR components and regional jet engines

V2500 / CFM56 USM Markets: Increasing Competition for Quality Parts

  • Multiple teardown programmes active simultaneously are creating a more competitive USM market — good for buyers, challenging for sellers trying to maintain margin
  • Key beneficiaries: Airlines and independent MROs sourcing parts for mid-life A320-family and 737NG fleet maintenance can increasingly find quality USM alternatives to expensive OEM new parts
  • Why it matters: As new OEM part lead times stretch to 18–24 months for some components, USM has become a primary supply channel — not a secondary fallback. The USM market is now a structural pillar of the MRO supply chain

Major Challenges and Solutions

Trent 500 and AE3007 Aftermarket: Niche but Significant Segments Activated

  • The Executive Jet Support A340-600 teardowns will produce Rolls-Royce Trent 500 components — a relatively niche but high-value market serving the remaining A340 fleet (~100+ aircraft still in commercial service)
  • The ERJ-145 teardowns activate the Allison/Rolls-Royce AE3007 market — critical for regional carriers in North America and Europe running ERJ-135/145/Legacy 600 aircraft
  • Challenge: As fleet sizes shrink, aftermarket support for "orphaned" types (A340-600, early ERJ variants) becomes increasingly difficult to source through OEM channels — driving reliance on teardown USM
  • Solution pathway: Purpose-structured teardown programmes like Executive Jet Support's model fill the OEM aftermarket gap for declining-fleet types
