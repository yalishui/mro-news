Daily MRO Market News Summary — March 20, 2026

March 20, 2026

Source: Aviation Week Network

Compiled from Aviation Week Network and leading industry sources | Focus period: March 17–20, 2026

The week of March 17–20 delivered a dense slate of MRO activity: a landmark China engine deal by Lufthansa Technik, a major U.S. facility inauguration by Safran, India's state MRO arm announcing two new greenfield hangars, and the Iran conflict entering the industry risk calculus for the first time. Structurally, the Oliver Wyman 2026–2036 forecast (released this week in executive-summary form) raised the 10-year MRO spend figure to a near-$200B horizon, while a Cirium webinar showed Asia-Pacific consolidating its dominance in heavy maintenance. Market Updates and Growth Trends

  • Oliver Wyman 2026–2036 Fleet & MRO Market Forecast (released via ARSA, ~March 16): Global MRO spend reached $136 billion in 2025 (up 8% from $126B in 2024) and is forecast to approach $193 billion by the end of the 2030s — nearly double the 2019 pre-pandemic baseline. The global active commercial fleet (ex-Russia) stands at ~30,000 aircraft and is projected to reach ~41,000 by 2036 (CAGR: 3.2%). Outstanding orders total ~17,000 aircraft, representing over 12 years of backlog at current production rates.
  • Fleet average age has risen to nearly 13 years (up ~1.5 years since 2024), directly driving MRO demand upward.
  • India leads all markets in fleet growth rate (CAGR ~7.1%); China leads in absolute volume; Middle East at ~5% CAGR.
  • 2025 passenger demand hit 5.2 billion passengers and RPKs were 9% above the 2019 pre-COVID peak — yet aircraft supply constraints prevent capacity from keeping pace.
  • Cirium Webinar: "MRO Market Shakeup: Unpacking the 2025 Global Trends" (March 18): Published insights confirm Asia-Pacific now handles 50–70% of global heavy maintenance work and holds a 56% share of the widebody C-check market.
  • MRO line capacity globally grew 5% from 2019–2025. Europe grew +28%; North America fell -20%.
  • Market concentration is tightening: just 35 MRO providers are expected to handle ~50% of all heavy events in 2025.
  • Narrow-body maintenance is more balanced: North America 34%, Asia 34%, Europe 28%.
  • Latin America identified as an emerging narrowbody MRO market.
  • Aviation Week "Fast 5" — AFI KLM E&M LEAP Strategy (March 19): AFI KLM Engineering & Maintenance is accelerating LEAP-1A and LEAP-1B MRO capacity, with upgraded test cell facilities in Paris (CDG) and Amsterdam (AMS) now fully operational for comprehensive LEAP performance testing. The company received its first LEAP-1B spare engine in Amsterdam in February 2026, marking progress on a commitment to purchase up to 10 LEAP spare engines to eliminate AOG disruption for premier MRO customers.

Regional Expansion Activities

  • Lufthansa Technik × Juneyao Group — Landmark China CFM56 Deal (signed ~March 17–18): LHT signed an exclusive, long-term comprehensive engine overhaul agreement with China's Juneyao Group covering 40+ engine events across two carriers — Juneyao Air (legacy) and 9 Air (LCC subsidiary). Engine types: CFM56-5B and CFM56-7B. Services include full overhaul, engine condition monitoring, and engineering consultancy, all performed at LHT's Hamburg facility. This is described as LHT's largest-ever engine services commitment from a Chinese customer and the first full engine overhaul partnership between the two companies despite a 10-year component/line-services relationship.
  • HAECO × ANA Line Maintenance Extension to 2030 (announced March 17): HAECO extended its line maintenance partnership with All Nippon Airways (ANA) in Hong Kong through 2030, covering comprehensive technical and non-technical services for ANA's Boeing 787, 767, and 777F fleet. The relationship spans more than two decades and is a flagship long-haul widebody line-maintenance contract for HAECO at Hong Kong International Airport.
  • Air Mauritius × AFI KLM E&M A350 Agreement (announced March 19): Air Mauritius signed a multi-year extension with AFI KLM E&M for A350 component and APU pool support, ensuring continued coverage for its widebody long-haul fleet. The deal reflects growing African and Indian Ocean airline reliance on European MRO majors for next-generation widebody support.
  • AIESL — India Greenfield MRO at Noida (Jewar) & Navi Mumbai (announced March 19): AI Engineering Services Ltd (AIESL, India's state-owned MRO) announced plans to invest ₹200–300 crore (~$24–36M USD) in new MRO facilities at two under-construction airports:
  • Noida International Airport (Jewar): One widebody hangar (~₹100–150 crore)
  • Navi Mumbai International Airport: One widebody hangar (~₹100–150 crore)
  • Both facilities are estimated to be operational within two years, subject to land acquisition and regulatory approvals.
  • Context: India's MRO market is projected to grow from $1.7B (2021) to over $4B by 2031, yet approximately 90% of heavy maintenance is still outsourced to Singapore, Dubai, and Colombo.
  • AIESL currently maintains ~20 aircraft/day across 6 bases (Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai, Kolkata, Nagpur, Thiruvananthapuram), including 5–6 widebodies.
  • GA Telesis Engine Services (GATES) — Mongolia CAAC CFM56 Certification (announced March 18): Finland-based GATES received certification from Mongolia's Civil Aviation Authority, establishing a new MRO partnership with MIAT Mongolian Airlines for CFM56-7B engine overhaul support. Adds to GATES' growing list of regulatory approvals (FAA, EASA, CAAC, TCCA, GACA).
  • Safran Electrical & Power — Sarasota, FL Facility Inauguration (March 18): Safran inaugurated an expanded MRO campus in Sarasota, Florida now totaling 140,000 sq ft, consolidating all electrical systems MRO activities from the former Thales Orlando site (acquired October 2023). Services cover generators, power distribution/protection, aircraft emergency systems, rotating machinery, and lithium battery systems. Facility originally established in 1978; Safran supports ~11,000 U.S. jobs across 25 states.

Supply Chain Developments

  • IATA × IATP Joint Supply Chain Strategy (announced March 16, following IATP Malta conference, March 7–11): In a landmark first, IATA and the International Airlines Technical Pool (IATP) formalized a joint strategy to address systemic MRO supply chain dysfunction. Key findings:
  • 2025 supply chain disruptions cost the airline industry an estimated $11.3 billion, broken down as:
  • $4.2B — excess fuel burn from delayed fleet retirements
  • $3.1B — extra maintenance costs
  • $2.6B — additional lease costs
  • $1.4B — excess service costs
  • Four-pillar mitigation strategy:

1. Open up aftermarket options (reduce OEM exclusivity barriers)

2. Expand use of PMA and alternative certified parts

3. Improve parts demand forecasting (tackle manufacturer-side opacity)

4. Strengthen anti-counterfeit part certification programs

  • IATA's Board has approved phased (short/medium/long-term) action documents. Next steps involve regulatory engagement and OEM collaboration.
  • Magnetic Group CFM Engine MRO Expansion vs. Bottlenecks (Aviation Week, March 17): Estonia-based Magnetic Engines is scaling up heavier CFM56 workscopes (including core performance restoration) and plans to launch in-house LEAP-1A/1B quick-turn shop visits from Tallinn by 2027. However, the company flagged acute constraints:
  • Engine test cell scarcity in Europe: Engines sent to third-party facilities face 4–6 month queues.
  • Hot-section parts shortage: Life-limited parts and thermal hardware for LEAP face 7–10 month shop visit cycles due to limited used serviceable material (USM).
  • Balancing machine coming in-house Q2 2026 will enable fully self-contained CFM56 overhaul.
  • Trend: MRO shops shifting capacity from CFM56 to LEAP is reducing CFM56 availability, echoing the 2017–2019 squeeze.
  • SkySelect AI Procurement Platform — $9M Funding (March 12–13, ongoing coverage this week): Estonian-founded SkySelect, an AI-powered aircraft parts procurement platform, raised $9 million (co-led by Verb Ventures and RockCreek) to expand its sourcing and procurement optimization tools. The platform uses specialized AI to help airlines and MRO providers reduce AOG delays and excess inventory by optimizing sourcing across fragmented global supply chains.

Major Challenges and Solutions

  • Iran Conflict — Limited but Monitored MRO Impact (Aviation Week / RBC Capital Markets, March 19): RBC Capital Markets analysts assessed that the ongoing Iran conflict does not yet represent a major MRO demand threat, but identified near-term risks:
  • Sales of certain non-serialized spare parts and short-cycle commercial aftermarket activity face pressure.
  • Middle Eastern carriers may delay payments, defer scheduled maintenance, or adjust inventory.
  • Industry fundamentals remain supportive: Load factor at 84%, global fleet average age 14.6 years (per RBC data), new aircraft supply severely constrained — airlines have little incentive to accelerate retirements.
  • Risk escalation threshold: Sustained conflict over multiple months + persistent high fuel prices would be needed to trigger significant MRO demand reduction or early retirement waves.
  • Some carriers (e.g., British Airways) are adding long-haul capacity to capture displaced passengers, potentially adding to MRO demand.
  • Engine Turnaround Time / Test Cell Scarcity (Magnetic Group, Aviation Week March 17): Engine TAT continues to be the #1 operational risk, with European MRO shops facing 4–6 month waits for third-party test cell access. The transition of capacity from CFM56 to LEAP is creating secondary supply gaps for CFM56 operators. Solutions being pursued: in-house test infrastructure investment, modular swap strategies, strategic USM/LLP pre-positioning.
  • Skilled Labor and Structural Supply Constraints (Oliver Wyman Forecast, IATA/IATP Joint Strategy): Labor shortages — particularly for licensed engineers and technical specialists — remain structurally embedded. The retirements wave among air traffic controllers and experienced technicians is compounding production delays. Average fleet age of ~13 years is driving intensifying maintenance demand against a backdrop of insufficient workforce pipeline.
  • LEAP Engine Long Shop Visit Cycles (Magnetic Group, March 17): LEAP-1A/1B shop visit turnaround currently averages 7–10 months due to parts scarcity. USM availability is thin because the engines are still relatively young in the fleet and few have been retired. This will be the dominant MRO headache of the late 2020s as LEAP shop visits peak.
  • India MRO Self-Sufficiency Gap (AIESL March 19): Despite a booming domestic aviation market (1,000+ aircraft on order), India still sends ~90% of heavy maintenance work overseas. AIESL's new Noida/Navi Mumbai hangar proposals and Akasa Air's domestic MRO facility (announced March 14) are part of a larger push to develop indigenous capability. Financial viability concerns remain as AIESL's maintenance revenue has shown slight decline against rising competition from airline-internal MRO divisions.
