PM Modi Inaugurates Jewar Airport Phase 1; Announces Rs 1,000 Crore MRO Hub with Akasa Air

  • Prime Minister Narendra Modi inaugurated Phase 1 of Noida International Airport (Jewar) on Saturday March 28, 2026 — marking a major milestone in India's airport expansion programme (APAC News Network, March 28)
  • MRO announcement: PM Modi announced development of a Rs 1,000 crore (~$120 million) MRO ecosystem at Jewar airport, in partnership with Akasa Air
  • Strategic vision: India aims to reduce its ~85% overseas MRO dependency, retain maintenance revenue domestically, create youth employment, and position the country as a global aircraft maintenance centre
  • Jewar Airport context: Located in Noida, near Delhi — designed to serve as a dual-hub alongside Indira Gandhi International Airport for Delhi NCR, India's largest aviation market
  • Akasa Air MRO facility: Akasa Air's first dedicated MRO base (strategic agreement signed March 27); will anchor the broader Jewar MRO hub ecosystem
  • Employment creation: MRO hub expected to generate significant skilled engineering and technical employment for youth in the Delhi NCR region
  • Economic multiplier: Full operational MRO hub will attract international aircraft to Jewar for maintenance, ensuring foreign-currency maintenance revenue flows into India rather than abroad
  • Political significance: Jewar project backed by both central government and Uttar Pradesh state government (CM Yogi Adityanath); positions UP as India's next major aviation investment destination

Supply Chain Developments

India Engine MRO: Capacity Investment Critical as Fleet Doubles

  • Engine MRO remains India's largest single MRO cost category and the segment most dependent on overseas providers (Alton Aviation, March 28)
  • Current challenge: India has minimal indigenous engine overhaul capacity for CFM56, LEAP-1A/1B, and V2500 engines that power its dominant narrowbody fleet
  • Investment response:
  • Safran's Hyderabad LEAP MRO facility (€200M) represents the most significant single engine MRO investment in India to date
  • Air India/TATA engineering divisions exploring domestic engine shop capability
  • Parts logistics gap: India's MRO parts supply chain remains fragmented — customs clearance delays and lack of bonded AOG parts warehousing increase turn times versus Singapore or Dubai competitors
  • Policy fix in progress: Reduced import duties on aviation components (implemented in recent budgets) and GIFT City aircraft leasing structures improving overall aviation supply chain economics

Source: Alton Aviation Consultancy, March 28, 2026

Industry Outlook

1. India Has Crossed the MRO Investment Threshold. The combination of PM Modi's Rs 1,000 crore Jewar announcement, Alton Aviation's $5.7B 2030 projection, and Akasa Air's formal MRO facility agreement — all on a single Saturday — marks a qualitative shift in India's MRO narrative. This is no longer a market "with potential" but a market in active capital deployment. The critical execution question now centres on three bottlenecks: engine MRO capability (Safran's Hyderabad facility is necessary but insufficient alone), customs/logistics reform (duty reductions are in progress but bonded warehouse infrastructure lags), and workforce quality (India produces engineering graduates in volume but lacks depth in specialised aircraft maintenance certification pathways).

2. The MRO Americas Agenda Reflects Global Industry Anxiety. The April conference agenda — tariffs, AI compliance, workforce shortage, supply chain fragility — reads as a diagnostic of 2026's core MRO tensions. Aviation Week's forecast of a $1.6 trillion cumulative demand opportunity over 10 years alongside a 20% workforce shortfall is the fundamental paradox the industry must solve. Capital is available; certified hands are not. The conference's new TechHub and AI-matching features signal an industry that recognises the answer is technology augmentation, not labour substitution alone.

3. In-House Maintenance Investment is Accelerating Across Airline Tiers. From Ryanair's £40M Prestwick expansion to Air India's line maintenance network build-out to Akasa's greenfield MRO facility, airlines are increasingly choosing to internalise maintenance capability rather than rely solely on third-party MROs. The economic logic is compelling: in-house maintenance reduces TAT, eliminates third-party margin, improves fleet availability, and builds proprietary engineering expertise. This trend, if sustained, will progressively redistribute MRO revenue from standalone MRO providers toward airline-internal engineering divisions.
